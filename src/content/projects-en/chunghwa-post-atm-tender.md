Connecting a century of postal trust across every town in Taiwan. Over three months (November 2023 - January 2024), I served as Project Manager Assistant, responsible for proposal writing, presentation production, and schedule planning, for Chunghwa Post Co., Ltd.

![Key visual of the Chunghwa Post ATM interface design tender](/legacy/project3/media33.png)

## Project Overview

- **Team goal**: Aligned with Chunghwa Post's digital development roadmap, the tender aimed to extend a century of postal trust and build a diverse, inclusive, and sustainable postal ecosystem — redesigning the new ATM visual style and interaction experience from the user's perspective.
- **Role and deliverables**: As Project Manager Assistant, I worked closely with one researcher, two designers, and two project leads, owning proposal writing, presentation production, and schedule planning — from tender strategy ideation to the final oral-defense retrospective.
- **Challenges**: The company was unfamiliar with financial-ATM business and the tender timeline was tight; both project leads were also occupied with other projects, making real-time communication and coordination difficult.
- **Results and impact**: The team not only completed the tender but placed 2nd. Through the retrospective, we optimized the company's proposal deck and further distilled tender guidelines and a standard process.

## Background

- **ATM market**: According to Financial Supervisory Commission statistics, Taiwan had 32,952 ATMs as of the end of October 2023 — a density three times the global average — showing how much Taiwanese users need and rely on ATMs. Research shows that simplified information architecture, efficient operation time and steps, and diverse, flexible functions all affect user satisfaction.
- **Inclusive-service trend**: In 2023 the FSC added a "Friendly Service Principle" to its Treating Customers Fairly principles for financial services, pushing financial institutions to treat elderly customers and people with disabilities fairly — putting inclusive ATM design in the spotlight.
- **Chunghwa Post's positioning**: Chunghwa Post has long centered the needs of customers of all ages and groups, with a digital roadmap aimed at "improving operational efficiency, optimizing customer experience, and innovating business models." As Taiwan's largest deposit institution, it holds many people's first savings account, with users spanning demographics and generations.

## Design Process

1. **Understand project needs and the client's status quo**: Deconstruct the tender document's key points and take stock of the target state through desk research and field investigation.
2. **Distill user research and problem challenges**: Synthesize user insights, map the difficulties and bottlenecks, and extract the key pain points.
3. **Ideate solution strategy and design output**: Converge on a core concept, position the proposal direction, and produce design concepts.
4. **Retrospective and standardization of the tender process**: Reflect on the process to improve efficiency and consistency, refining the workflow to reduce errors and learning costs.

## User Research

During the proposal stage, we quantified findings through surveys to understand how different user types behave at ATMs — their needs and pain points — and planned qualitative interviews with key stakeholders for after the award. The research covered four groups: "students and part-time workers (under 24)" who are unfamiliar with ATMs and less satisfied; "young budget-conscious strivers (25-38)" who are sensitive to interface aesthetics and only visit ATMs for specific needs; "middle-aged family builders (39-50)" who care about operation flow and show high brand loyalty; and "mature pre-retirees (51-60+)" who prefer physical machines and struggle with new features.

![User quotes and trait tags across the four user groups](/legacy/project3/media39.png)

- **Students and part-time workers**: "The screen doesn't show how to insert the card, so I often insert it the wrong way." "I can't find the function I need, and I can't go back a level, so I just eject the card and cancel." "It's hard to tell which bank's machine dispenses NT$100 bills."
- **Middle-aged family builders**: "The ATM flow is long — after withdrawing there are still coupon and marketing pages, which gets tiresome." "(Cathay United Bank) lets you choose fast withdrawal right at the start; it feels convenient and quick." "The flow feels generic and dated."

## Solution Strategy and Expected Benefits

![Three strategic directions: innovation, diversity, usability](/legacy/project3/media19.png)

- **Innovation**: The future ATM should connect Chunghwa Post's four flows — money, logistics, commerce, and information — becoming the "offline integrated touchpoint of the four postal businesses," complementing the mobile app and i-Post Box smart lockers to grow the postal ecosystem's multi-service platform.
- **Diversity**: For most people in Taiwan, their first account is with Chunghwa Post. Students, migrant workers, rural residents, and financially underserved groups depend heavily on post-office ATMs, so a friendly, inclusive, and accessible experience is essential.
- **Usability**: Focus on "improving operational efficiency," "keeping users focused on their task," and "optimizing error-prevention and recovery mechanisms" to comprehensively raise ATM usability.

## Design Output

**01 | Innovation: an inclusive, extensible home-screen layout** — Starting from the needs of diverse user groups, the layout balances efficiency, extensibility, and accessibility: quick-withdrawal options on the home screen with stronger recognition of the withdrawal function; a new post-office zone and campaign area integrating Chunghwa Post's signature services into a one-stop layout with more flexibility; and accessibility optimizations that place basic settings in the footer area, within the height and comfortable reach of wheelchair users.

![Annotated redesigned home screen: quick-withdrawal block, account overview, post-office zone, accessibility entry](/legacy/project3/media21.png)

**02 | Diversity: a color scheme friendly to everyone** — Considering users with color-vision deficiency and low vision, we replanned a friendlier, more readable interface palette, simulating what color-blind users would see before finalizing the design to ensure it stays readable and operable.

![Interface palette validated through color-blindness simulations](/legacy/project3/media22.png)

**03 | Usability: a fraud-protection mechanism balanced across users** — Weighing the scenarios of ordinary users against scam victims, the design lets ordinary users transfer smoothly while surfacing the most critical signal — the decrease in account balance — giving victims a chance to notice and cancel the transaction.

![Anti-fraud transfer flow: users must check the balance change before confirming](/legacy/project3/media23.png)

The team also built a design system unifying colors, typography, components, design elements, cards, and icons to keep the design consistent across screens.

![Design system: color palette, typography, components, and icons](/legacy/project3/media24.png)

## Results

Compared against the old version, the outcome upholds three design principles: consistency, hierarchy, and accessibility.

![Showcase of the redesigned ATM interface](/legacy/project3/media25.jpg)

- **Consistency**: Fusing the symbolism of Chunghwa Post's irregular rounded corners and diagonal color blocks strengthens the brand image, aligns with its digital-transformation ambitions, creates word-of-mouth effects for the ATM business, and differentiates it from competitors.
- **Hierarchy**: Keeping users focused on their task, optimizing each page's information hierarchy, and highlighting the key action at every step reduces confusion and the chance of getting stuck.
- **Accessibility**: New transaction reference information (reference fees, today's withdrawal amount and limit, account balance, NT$100-bill availability, and more) reduces information anxiety during withdrawals and improves flow guidance.

## Reflections and Learnings

After the tender, the team ran a deep retrospective across three dimensions.

### Tender Timeline

- **Early stage**: Icebreakers and writing down expectations for the project's impact built cohesion; planning ahead from macro scheduling down to micro proposal reviews; running small internal studies first, then distilling the literature so the proposal carried stronger evidence.
- **Bidding stage**: Emphasize the scoring criteria and plan work-item weights and priorities; keep the proposal and the presentation narrative consistent — a deck that works as a "guided tour of the proposal" is ideal; make responsibilities explicit with full team participation.
- **Presentation stage**: Keep management and executors in sync on framing and documents; de-risk early by inviting experts and outside groups to review and run mock defenses; rehearse the pitch and the unified Q&A repeatedly.

### Work and Communication

- **Begin with the end in mind**: Plan backward from the project goal — build the ability to infer the client's true purpose for launching the project, and when frontline and leadership visions diverge, proactively establish internal consensus.
- **Information transparency**: Project context should be handed down — open information channels so newcomers understand the project's history; upgrade document collaboration to real-time tools to cut alignment and revision time.

### Mindset

- **Business thinking**: Beyond knowing design, know how to make clients buy into it — position for diverse audiences with genuine empathy for the client, build business-strategy and management literacy, and converge, consolidate, and apply results after dividing the work.
- **Management thinking**: Pair diligent craft with decisive management — proactively surface blind spots to avoid tunnel vision, tailor the management approach to each kind of project, and ground staffing, scheduling, and risk control in evidence rather than gut feel.
- **Partnership**: People are not machines; emotions need care too — managers also need recognition and encouragement; adopt a collaborative mindset to make the most of partners' resources and abilities; and widen the project's circle so more people can contribute ideas.
