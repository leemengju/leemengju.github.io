## Overview

Amid the global net-zero trend, IISI launched a "one-stop carbon management platform", entering the market with a carbon-inventory solution and partnering with UXI-Design to raise product value through experience design and secure a foothold in the carbon-management market.

- **Role**: UXUI designer — responsible for part of the inventory and system-feature wireframes and GUI/mockups, plus organizing and presenting the company-internal sharing session
- **Duration**: six months
- **Client**: International Integrated Systems, Inc. (IISI)
- **Design partner**: UXI-Design
- **Team**: worked closely with two project managers and three designers, joining mid-project and carrying it through to final development handoff

The challenges: the company was unfamiliar with the carbon-inventory domain, requirements shifted significantly under a tight schedule, both parties' project managers were replaced mid-way, and the partner designers were relatively junior with limited large-project experience. The team still broke away from the visual clichés common to ESG products and introduced a more usable, comprehensive UX operating logic; through an internal sharing session we exchanged the difficulties and pain points of the engagement and refined the checkpoints and standard process for mid-to-large projects.

## Background

Facing a gradually saturating carbon-product market, the strategy of "setting the experience benchmark" let a latecomer break through effectively.

Under the global net-zero trend, Taiwan set a policy of reaching net-zero carbon emissions by 2050. The market filled with physical and digital solutions for emission control, and the digital space already offered many carbon-management systems and software tailored to different company sizes and needs. As a latecomer, IISI adopted the "one-stop carbon management platform" as its product strategy, choosing the starting point of the carbon-management life cycle — a carbon-inventory solution — as its market debut. To fully exploit the latecomer's advantage, IISI partnered with UXI-Design, using experience design to raise product value and formally establish itself in the carbon-management market. (Related coverage: [iThome press release](https://www.ithome.com.tw/pr/159730))

## Design Strategy

Combining deep system-optimization experience with the unique needs of carbon inventory to quickly deliver a complete design for the client.

The biggest challenge: within a compressed four-month schedule, turn 16 carbon-inventory step modules and 50+ feature flows — from scratch — into 700+ pages of system UI that fit enterprise scenarios. The team worked in an agile manner, analyzed the pain points of current carbon-inventory workflows, ran competitive research, and folded the distinctive needs of carbon-inventory users into customized feature operations and UI presentation.

![The five steps of a carbon inventory: define boundaries, identify emission sources, calculate emissions, produce the inventory report, third-party verification](/legacy/project2/media15.png)

Combining the stakeholder interviews from the project's early phase, we distilled three main design goals:

**1. An efficiency-first inventory workflow**

Learning from enterprise carbon-inventory consultants, we surfaced both visible and hidden pain points, consolidated internal flows and connected external data to achieve a "focus on the task, minimize context switching" experience. Design examples include the streamlined "add an activity-data record" flow and a movable built-in toolbox that reduces looking up and cross-checking official reference data.

![Design example: activity-data entry with built-in map and distance conversion, no need to leave the form](/legacy/project2/media07.png)

![Design example: batch-select activity-data records and supporting evidence, then link them in one click](/legacy/project2/media11.png)

<video controls muted playsinline src="/legacy/project2/media31.mp4"></video>

**2. Scenario-driven information display**

We inventoried the existing government form fields and re-planned the information hierarchy and chart presentation around "inventory task scenarios", covering three role contexts: front-line data entry (viewing detailed data per category), external verification (cross-checking data correctness), and executive reporting (plant-wide overview and insights) — adjusting the interface and system permissions per role.

![Design example: the permission matrix planned around role scenarios](/legacy/project2/media36.png)

**3. Painless, guided task operations**

Targeting companies that had just adopted carbon-management workflows and government inventory forms — those still needing consultant support — we added next-step hints and textual guidance to steps that could easily be misread, for example the Drawer interfaces for different emission-source data.

![Design example: activity-data Drawer interfaces customized per emission-source category](/legacy/project2/media43.png)

## Color Plan

Stepping away from the green and blue palettes common to ESG products, the system pairs "Innovation Purple" with "Vibrant Orange" as primary and secondary colors, countering the perception of carbon inventory as tedious and conveying novelty and energy.

- Innovation Purple (Primary Color): #5E65FF
- Vibrant Orange (Secondary Color): #FF7E07

![Color plan: the Innovation Purple and Vibrant Orange dual palette](/legacy/project2/media12.png)

## UI Style

A minimal style optimized for information legibility, extended from the color plan; decorative elements are added in proportion to each page's information type and weight, balancing the client's demand for visual distinctiveness.

UI Style: #clean-white-base #subtle-skeuomorphism #solid-color-accents #linear-dividers

![UI style overview: clean white base, subtle skeuomorphism, solid-color accents, linear dividers](/legacy/project2/media13.png)

Considering the context and purpose of each inventory step, information that needs comparison and cross-reference is presented as infographics, improving enterprise users' understanding of what the inventory data means.

## Information Architecture

We rethought the government-mandated form fields with future feature expansion in mind, restructured the information through task-relevance analysis, split the inventory workflow into vertical task layers, and dynamically disclose to-dos according to the current inventory stage.

Inventory stages: boundary setting → emission-source identification and overview → activity-data entry → emission calculation → data quality management → uncertainty assessment → inventory results → internal verification → reduction planning

## Wrap-up

Co-created with the client a user experience that surpasses competing products, empowering the product and amplifying its growth potential.

A carbon-inventory system can be more than a data-platform solution — it can be a powerful way for a company to show that it values employees' needs and cares about society and the environment. ## Reflections and Learnings

After the project we ran a retrospective and shared the difficulties and pain points at a company-internal session, grouping the issues into three dimensions — project management, multi-person collaboration, and tooling — and discussing them with senior designers and researchers to refine the design process for mid-to-large projects.

- **Complex client hierarchy and stakeholders, conflicting opinions hard to conclude**: map the client's hierarchy and final decision-maker early, collect differing opinions and present each option's pros and cons; keep doubtful proposals for confirmation at a second meeting instead of acting on impulse decisions; reach an internal verdict first and recommend one option strategically; or present one option and keep the rest as alternates (e.g., the table-versus-card choice).
- **Managing internal drafts and the discard area**: regularly tidy discussion traces and keep only the final pages and flows; organize the discard area by date/stage/feature.
- **Managing project comments**: routinely check that comments on every page are resolved; or keep resolved comments so PMs and engineers can trace how features evolved; when necessary, create a separate "final delivery version" for the client.
- **Communication gaps from split meetings and per-page component additions**: set up a GUI/guideline notice board to unify site-wide logic; split meetings by purpose, running "feature discussion" and "information alignment" in parallel; keep communication short and frequent — with more than four designers, add a brief daily sync.
