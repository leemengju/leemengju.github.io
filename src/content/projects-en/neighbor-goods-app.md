## Project Overview

**01 | Team goal**: This project was the capstone of the 2nd online bootcamp at AAPD product design academy. With online trading diversifying and environmental awareness rising, our team chose "second-hand trading" as the theme and set out to design a localized online-trading app.

**02 | Role and deliverables**: I served as PM and UXUI designer, working closely with three other designers. Together we completed the overall product ideation and production, while I personally focused on scheduling as well as the final presentation deck and delivery.

**03 | Challenges**: The schedule was extremely tight, and the team members were relatively junior and all held full-time jobs, which made time management a major difficulty. The team had to frequently align on availability, progress and the mentors' design reviews.

**04 | Outcome**: The team not only finished on time but also earned high praise from the mentors. Through their feedback and our retrospectives, we came to understand the difficulties and keys of remote collaboration and rapid design sprints, and iterated further to make the project more thorough and refined.

## Background

Understanding the development goals, market landscape and current challenges around second-hand trading.

**The issue**: Second-hand trading is a long-standing human practice that remains meaningful in modern society. According to the UN Sustainable Development Goals, it helps end poverty, improve quality of life, drive economic growth and reduce inequality; it also supports sustainable cities and communities and responsible production-consumption cycles, aligned with the global long-term vision of sustainable development.

**Market trends**: Taiwan's second-hand market is increasingly active across many product categories, and is especially popular with young consumers who prefer practical, reasonably priced goods and value convenient transactions. With rising environmental awareness and economic uncertainty, more people are turning to second-hand platforms, and some platforms have innovated barter-style exchange models that emphasize resource reuse.

![Trend analysis of Taiwan's second-hand trading market](/legacy/project1/media10.png)

**Problem research**: Second-hand trading faces several problems — product authenticity and quality are hard to guarantee, and counterfeits and fraud erode consumer trust; as platforms multiply, competition grows homogeneous and merchants must differentiate; second-hand goods generally lack after-sales service, making high-priced items especially risky; and some platforms suffer from poor UX with inadequate search and filtering, making shopping difficult.

![Four key problems in the second-hand trading market](/legacy/project1/media11.png)

## Product Framework

Based on the background and problem research, the team framed the product to address the market's pain points.

**CIRCLES + 5W1H product statement**: Using the CIRCLES method, we defined the product as an exchange platform for second-hand furniture and daily goods, designed for renters, students and users who care about home styling. Users can access the platform anytime, anywhere, publishing or filtering listings through the app and finding suitable second-hand items by location, price and style — simplifying moving and disposal of belongings.

**Competitive analysis**: The team analyzed four mainstream second-hand platforms — Xianyu, Ruten, Carousell and Tsohbin — comparing each platform's core value, target audience, strengths, weaknesses and feature sets. Xianyu emphasizes community interaction, Ruten focuses on diverse product categories, Carousell stresses instant trading, and Tsohbin centers on local-community convenience. Each has a distinct position, and their differences in features and customer experience provided valuable reference for our product development.

**Market positioning**: With interface simplicity/richness as the X axis and customization/mass-market appeal as the Y axis, we mapped competitors into four quadrants. Per our product statement, this product positions itself in the "easy to operate, approachable features" quadrant, emphasizing simplicity and broad accessibility.

![Market positioning map: easy to operate, approachable features](/legacy/project1/media14.png)

## Audience Research

To validate the product hypotheses, the team first mapped the platform's key stakeholders and narrowed the target group, then used qualitative research to produce the persona, user stories and journey map that grounded the core strategy.

**Stakeholder mapping**: Second-hand trading involves many stakeholders; we visualized them in tiers by type and importance, and per the product statement narrowed the target group to "social" and "value- and practicality-oriented" buyers.

![Stakeholder map and target-audience convergence](/legacy/project1/media15.png)

**Persona & user story**: To understand how the target group buys second-hand furniture to furnish their homes, we interviewed nine participants, then converged the findings with affinity diagramming to build the persona and derive user stories.

![Persona: Sherry, a first-time renter — habits, pain points and goals](/legacy/project1/media16.png)

**Journey map**: Based on the product's core user flow, the team built a journey map to understand user behavior, motivations and pain points. We discovered the importance of data visualization and information hierarchy, and turned these into design opportunities.

## Core Strategy

Building on the audience research, the team distilled the brand philosophy and values, and converged on the product's MVP and iteration features.

**Brand wheel**: Placing the product concept into the brand-wheel framework yielded our core value — "building the heart's destination for second-hand trading with the beauty of locality." A localized brand core builds long-term customer trust and loyalty, forming a stable, consistent image in consumers' minds.

**Brand spirit — CMA**: We established three core values — Community, Mutual Trust and Amazingoods — extended into nine key imagery themes that shaped the brand's mood board, color palette and logo design. The platform emphasizes local mutual aid, offering high-quality practical furniture through transparent information and an honest trading environment, serving young renters and improving their living experience.

**Core strategy**: Recommend high value-for-money goods, raise information transparency, and promote localized trading through geolocation filtering; meanwhile strengthen transaction-protection mechanisms and blend in community lifestyle elements to enhance the shopping experience and user engagement — giving the design execution a clear thread to follow.

![Five core strategies: value-for-money recommendations, product transparency, localized trading, platform protection, community-infused trading](/legacy/project1/media22.png)

## Identity Definition

Distilling the mood board, color plan, logo and naming to establish the initial product CIS.

**Color plan**: The primary color "Cypress Brown" evokes phytoncides from nature, linking the warm, mutual-aid brand impression, and is used for primary controls and CTAs; the secondary "Swan Yellow," drawn from the warmth of a down duvet, reduces visual heaviness and enriches layering alongside the primary; the neutral "Steady Gray" serves as text and background color, balancing saturation and harmony.

![Color plan: Cypress Brown, Swan Yellow, Steady Gray](/legacy/project1/media24.png)

**Logo and naming**: The NEIGHBOR-GOODS logo embodies the threefold image of "good neighbors, good mutual aid, good goods." A lowercase n forms the main shape, with a figure-ground reversal embedding the g of "goods"; the rounded silhouette conveys a friendly community atmosphere, expressing the platform's commitment to affordable, reliable and convenient second-hand trading where good neighbors help each other and share good goods.

![Logo concept: neighbor + mutual help + gift](/legacy/project1/media25.png)

## Design Execution

We drew the IA and user flow to keep the design output anchored to the core strategy and issue research, and produced a design system and handoff documents to maintain development consistency.

**Information architecture & user flow**: The IA provides clear content organization and navigation structure; the user flow helps the team understand how users move through the product, using visualized behavior diagrams to identify and resolve potential issues and improve the coherence and fluency of the experience. The modular layout also aided internal division of work and discussion.

**Project scope and core flows**: The MVP covers six feature modules — locating and adjusting the product-search area; searching/filtering/browsing listings; a flash-sale zone; social Reels-style product short videos; seller information and contact; and the favorites list and checkout screens.

## Design Highlights

In the final design, the team proposed differentiating innovations against market competitors, tying back to the core strategy and the problem research:

1. **Flash-sale section** — delivers an economical shopping experience at better value-for-money prices, with multiple sale windows to boost participation.
2. **Optimized product information** — listings require real-appearance videos and photos, product tags, transaction and seller information, and a Q&A section, so users better understand an item's actual condition.
3. **Location filtering** — offers geolocation, manual input and district filtering, making it easy to trade with nearby sellers and flexibly view and filter the trading range.
4. **Optimized seller information** — the interface surfaces a seller's past product categories, reputation ratings, activity level and on-time shipping rate, strengthening buyers' transaction safety.
5. **The "Live" tab** — embeds social-media elements into the platform to match users' casual-browsing habits; buyers can chat with the seller directly, make an offer and complete the purchase.

## Retrospective

Finally, we reviewed the project's overall arc through an hourglass model: the team first explored the issue's status quo and problems in the background phase, then established the product's characteristics and project scope through the framework; audience research validated the product hypotheses and informed the core strategy; and from those core values we defined the visual design direction and executed the concrete design. The whole project was built by 4 designers across 16 meetings, 21 work items and 288 hours.

### Improvement suggestions

**01 | Balancing the project, life and work**: Set a clear time-management plan to avoid excessive anxiety; prioritize, keep communication with teammates effective and flexible, and don't hold back encouragement for each other at key milestones.

**02 | Balancing project management and collaboration blind spots**: Everyone owns their own part, but some work items can overlap — increasing communication frequency raises the completeness of the whole project, and a shared pre-submission checklist lets the team verify everything together.

**03 | Fatigue in the late stages of a tight schedule**: A bootcamp aims to level up participants quickly in a short time, which can easily overload junior designers; trimming redundant, time-consuming content according to the schedule makes it easier to stay focused on the project's core.
